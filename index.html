AFRICAN PLAINS

Pan-Africanism is a sociopolitical philosophy and movement that promotes solidarity, togetherness, and collaboration among people of African origin, both on the African continent and in the African diaspora across the world. It originated in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as a reaction against colonialism, racism, and exploitation of African peoples.

Key principles of Pan-Africanism include:

African Unity: Promotes political and economic unity among African states and peoples, believing that collective action is required to confront common difficulties and make progress.

Self-determination: Supports African peoples' freedom to choose their own destiny free of external intervention or oppression.

Unity: Emphasizes the necessity of African unity and collaboration, regardless of geography or national identity.

Cultural identity: The celebration and promotion of African culture, tradition, and values, with the goal of reclaiming and asserting African identity.

Social justice: Strives to eliminate racial injustice, inequality, and exploitation in African nations and across the world.

Marcus Garvey

Marcus Garvey was a political activist, businessman, and leader from Jamaica who rose to prominence in the Pan-Africanism movement. He passed away in London, England on June 10, 1940, after being born in St. Ann's Bay, Jamaica, on August 17, 1887.

W.E.B. Du Bois

Born William Edward Burghardt Du Bois on February 23, 1868, W.E.B. Du Bois was a Pan-Africanist, author, editor, historian, sociologist, and campaigner for civil rights for African Americans. He is regarded as one of the most significant thinkers of the 20th century and is well-known for his activism, research, and support of social justice and racial equality.

Kwame Nkrumah

Politician and revolutionary Kwame Nkrumah (1909–1972) was a key figure in Ghana's fight for independence from British colonial authority. He was educated both domestically in Ghana and overseas, notably in the United States and the United Kingdom, after being born in Nkroful, Gold Coast (now in Ghana).

Patrice Lumumba

Patrice Lumumba was a Congolese politician and independence leader who played a significant role in the decolonization of Congo from Belgian rule. Born on July 2, 1925, in the Belgian Congo (now the Democratic Republic of Congo), Lumumba became involved in anti-colonial activism during his early adulthood.

Emperor Haile Selassie

From 1930 to 1974, Haile Selassie ruled Ethiopia as its emperor. On July 23, 1892, in the tiny Ethiopian Empire village of Ejersa Goro, he was born under the name Tafari Makonnen. He was a well-known figure in Ethiopian politics and society, and throughout his rule, there were many difficulties and important developments.

Dr Kihura Nkuba

Ugandan physician, radio host, and political activist Dr. Kihura Nkuba is well-known for his candid opinions on human rights and politics in his home country. He has long been a well-known personality in Ugandan politics and media.

Although pan-African integration has advanced, a mental shift is still required.

The Africa Day celebration this year offers yet another chance to evaluate the extent of continental integration's advancement. A completely unified Africa would result from integration; this may take the form of the African Union (AU) imposing binding laws on its member states or a federalist "United States of Africa." Currently, the 55 member states of the AU are only able to coordinate their interactions through this platform. Even though there has been considerable progress, more work has to be done to accomplish integration. Member states must give vital AU organs more authority and stop just lip service to unity. This calls for a mental adjustment. States must recognize that certain autonomy must be given up in order to achieve shared socioeconomic and political objectives.

How Kwame Nkrumah established a custom for commemorating the moment of freedom with his midnight address

In honor of Ghana's 65th anniversary of independence from Great Britain, it is appropriate to review Kwame Nkrumah's historic address, which he gave at midnight on the day Ghana was founded. In the anticolonial fight, Nkrumah spearheaded a popular movement calling for self-government, and once Ghana gained independence, he was set to become the country's first prime minister. The first nation in sub-Saharan Africa to be freed from colonial authority was Ghana. Consequently, Nkrumah's address at the time of Ghana's liberation established a spirit of pride in the country's achievements as well as optimism for the ongoing freedom battles across decolonizing Africa and its diaspora.

How Swahili rose to prominence as the most widely spoken language in Africa

Swahili, formerly only a little-known island variant of the Bantu language of Africa, has developed into the continent's most well recognized language. It is comparable to the select handful languages in the world with more than 200 million speakers. The story's shapers—immigrants from inland Africa, traders from Asia, Arab and European occupiers, European and Indian settlers, colonial rulers, and people from various postcolonial nations—have used and modified Swahili over the course of its two millennia of development and adaptation. They have brought it with them on all of their western travels. The Swahili-speaking region of Africa currently encompasses the entirety of the continent, spanning from south to north and touching on the opposite shore.

Several African nations have noteworthy independent publishing scenes. South Africa, Zimbabwe, Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania are a few of these. Nonetheless, a variety of risks have always been present in this delicate business. First among them is the lax enforcement of copyright laws, which impedes future development. Undoubtedly, school textbooks provide these companies with steady revenue streams. However, this tends to stifle fiction and research publications.
